# The Competence Inversion

A friend of mine graduated last year with a computer science degree from a good university. Not elite, but good. She did everything right: internships, side projects, decent grades. She applied to 340 junior developer positions between September and February. She got four interviews. None converted.

The roles she was trained for still exist in job listings. But the listings now say "3-5 years experience" where they used to say "entry-level." The junior tier is collapsing — not because companies don't need software, but because an AI system that costs a few hundred dollars a month can produce passable code faster than a new graduate who costs a salary, benefits, and six months of ramp-up time. The economics aren't even close.

She's not incompetent. She's precisely competent — in exactly the way that is now least valuable.

---

I've been thinking about this for a while, and I want to lay out an argument that I find genuinely unsettling: that we spent the last fifty years building an education system optimised to produce people who are maximally replaceable by artificial intelligence.

This isn't a hostile takeover. It's a tragic alignment between what we taught people to be and what machines turned out to be better at. We trained people to process information reliably, follow complex instructions, produce consistent output, manage workflows, synthesise documents, and communicate in standardised formats. These are precisely the cognitive tasks that large language models perform at scale for near-zero marginal cost. We accidentally trained humans to be artisanal versions of something that just became industrial.

The irony is vicious. The people who did the best in the old system — who followed the rules most diligently, who were most consistent, who produced the most reliable output — are the most exposed. Not because they lack ability. Because the ability they have is the ability machines also have.

---

Alex Karp has been saying something provocative about who thrives in this world, and I think he's partly right and partly telling a story that flatters him. His argument is that two groups will be fine: people who work with their hands — electricians, plumbers, carpenters, people whose skills require physical presence and embodied judgment — and people who think differently. The lateral thinkers, the pattern-breakers, the people whose minds don't follow predictable paths. The neurodivergent. Everyone in the middle — the enormous population trained for conventional knowledge work — is stranded.

Karp credits his own dyslexia for forcing him to think in non-standard ways, and he's built a fellowship explicitly for neurodivergent thinkers. I find this framing both powerful and self-serving in ways that matter.

The powerful part: the education system has spent decades punishing exactly the cognitive profiles that might be most valuable in an AI-saturated economy. The kid who couldn't sit still, who gave answers that were technically wrong but conceptually interesting, who couldn't follow the standard process but occasionally produced something genuinely original — that kid was medicated, remediated, held back, and told they weren't meeting expectations. Now the expectations have changed, and the kids who met them are the ones in trouble.

The self-serving part: Karp is neurodivergent, runs a company that employs neurodivergent people, and is positioning neurodivergence as the scarce resource in an AI economy. That's a convenient story for a man whose entire professional identity is built on thinking differently from the consensus. It's also a story that creates a new hierarchy — one where being neurotypical becomes the disadvantage — without actually solving the structural problem. You can't retrain someone to be neurodivergent. If Karp's framing is correct, most people are just out of luck by virtue of how their brains are wired.

Daniela Amodei offers a more democratic version of essentially the same observation. Her argument is that the humanities — context, ambiguity, ethical judgment, the ability to navigate situations where there is no clear right answer — become more important, not less, in an AI-saturated world. The skills that English departments and philosophy programmes have been teaching, the ones that parents worried were "impractical," are precisely the skills that machines are worst at replicating. Interpreting what someone means in a politically sensitive email. Making a judgment call when the data is ambiguous and the stakes are human. Understanding why a technically optimal decision would be ethically catastrophic.

I find Daniela's framing more humane than Karp's because it doesn't require a specific neurological profile. It requires training — training that exists but that we systematically defunded and devalued for a generation. We told students that philosophy doesn't pay. We cut arts programmes. We measured educational success in STEM pipeline metrics. And now the STEM pipeline produces people who compete directly with machines, while the skills we dismissed as soft turn out to be the hard ones.

---

But here is the part that genuinely scares me, and where Dario Amodei's analysis cuts deeper than either his sister's or Karp's. In his essay on what he calls the adolescence of technology, he projects that fifty percent of entry-level white-collar jobs could be affected within one to five years. That number is staggering, but the mechanism is what keeps me up at night.

The standard reassurance about technological displacement is the "lump of labour fallacy" — the idea that there isn't a fixed amount of work, and that new technologies create new categories of jobs that absorb displaced workers. ATMs didn't eliminate bank tellers; they freed up tellers to do more complex advisory work. Spreadsheets didn't eliminate accountants; they freed up accountants to do more strategic analysis.

Amodei's point is that AI might break this pattern in a historically unprecedented way. Previous technologies were narrow: they automated specific tasks, which opened adjacent tasks for humans. But AI has general cognitive ability. When a new job category emerges — say, "AI prompt engineer" or "AI ethics consultant" — AI itself can learn to do that job within a few years, sometimes months. The escape hatch keeps closing. You find the thing humans are still better at, you build a career around it, and then a model update makes you redundant again.

This is what I mean by the competence inversion. It's not a one-time displacement where you retrain and recover. It's a treadmill. You run toward the frontier of human advantage, and the frontier retreats faster than you can chase it.

---

Gary Marcus adds a wrinkle here that I initially resisted but have come to think is essential. His argument is that economic displacement happens even when the AI doesn't work well. Companies deploy AI not because it's better than humans, but because it's cheaper. If an AI system produces work that's eighty percent as good as a junior employee at five percent of the cost, the economics are decisive. Quality degrades, but slowly enough that most customers don't notice, and most managers can report the cost savings to their board before the quality problems become visible.

This means the displacement isn't even contingent on AI being impressive. It's contingent on AI being cheap. A mediocre but inexpensive system displaces a competent but expensive human, and the competence gap gets absorbed as an externality — slightly worse products, slightly more errors, slightly less creativity — distributed so widely that no individual decision-maker bears the cost of having caused it. The labour market version of a tragedy of the commons.

I find this deeply uncomfortable because it strips away the comforting narrative that human quality will always command a premium. It might not. Not because humans aren't better, but because "better" isn't what the market selects for when the price differential is large enough.

---

There's a layer of this that I take personally, and I want to be honest about why.

I spent years in computational psychiatry studying how neurodivergent minds process information differently — not deficiently, but differently. The clinical literature still treats neurodivergence primarily as a set of deficits to be managed: attention deficits, social processing deficits, executive function deficits. The word "deficit" is doing enormous conceptual work, and most of it is wrong.

What ADHD actually looks like, in many cases, is a brain that cannot force itself to attend to things it finds uninteresting but enters states of extraordinary focus on things it finds meaningful. What autism actually looks like, in many cases, is a mind that processes information in ways that are less socially conventional but sometimes more logically rigorous, more pattern-sensitive, more honest. These are not deficits in a world that values original thinking, deep focus, and refusal to accept received wisdom. They are deficits only in a world that demands compliance, consistency, and the ability to perform cognitive tasks that a machine now performs better.

The competence inversion doesn't just change who is economically valuable. It retroactively reveals that the people we pathologised were, in some cases, the people least suited to being replaced by automation — precisely because they were least suited to the industrial model of cognitive work that automation replaces.

Karp is naming my population. But naming them is not the same as building for them. Recognising that neurodivergent thinkers might be valuable in an AI economy is not the same as creating the infrastructure that lets them actually thrive. The systems they need — systems that adapt to their cognitive patterns rather than demanding they adapt to neurotypical ones, systems that translate the world into their terms rather than requiring them to perform in someone else's — those systems largely don't exist yet.

---

But I need to be honest about something harder. The argument I've been making so far has a comforting shape: the skills that matter are human skills, the people with unusual minds are the ones who'll thrive, and if we just retrain and revalue, we'll be fine.

I'm not sure that's true.

If AI's cognitive ability is genuinely general — if it keeps expanding into new domains at anything like the rate it has over the past three years — then there may not be a stable category of "things humans are irreplaceably better at." Creativity was supposed to be safe. It wasn't. Emotional intelligence was supposed to be safe. AI companions are getting disturbingly good at it. Moral judgment was supposed to be safe. But most moral judgments in practice are pattern-matching against precedent, and pattern-matching is precisely what these systems do.

The thing I help someone discover about themselves — their unique cognitive pattern, their specific way of making sense of the world — AI might learn to replicate or approximate that pattern within a few years of me identifying it. Every time I say "this is what makes you irreplaceable," I might just be identifying the next capability that gets automated. It's not an escalator toward permanent human advantage. It might be a treadmill where the speed keeps increasing.

Andrew Ng's vision — that every person should build their own AI systems, that democratising AI access solves the displacement problem — is appealing but may be insufficient. Giving everyone a hammer doesn't help when there are no nails to drive. Access to tools matters only if there's work that the tools enable humans to do. If the tools can do the work without the humans, access is just a more egalitarian form of obsolescence.

And there's a version of Karp's argument that fails too. If robotics advances the way software has — and there are serious people who think it will — then the vocational workers who are supposedly safe become exposed as well. Embodied work provides a temporary refuge, not a permanent one.

---

This brings me to a question I don't have an answer to, and I think intellectual honesty requires me to say so.

If human economic productivity genuinely approaches zero — not for everyone, not all at once, but gradually, for an expanding population — what is the right response? The traditional answer is: help people become more productive, more skilled, more competitive. Augmentation. Make them better at the things that still matter. That's the answer I instinctively reach for, and it's the answer that feels actionable.

But there's a version of the future where augmentation isn't enough, because there's nothing left to augment toward. Where the question isn't "how do I compete?" but "how do I find meaning in a world that doesn't need my cognitive labour?" That's a different question entirely, and it requires a different kind of infrastructure — not tools that make you more economically competitive, but tools that help you understand who you are, what you value, and how to build a life that feels worthwhile independent of what the market says you're worth.

I notice that I've been conflating economic value and human value throughout this essay, and that conflation is itself part of the problem. We live in a culture that derives identity from work, purpose from productivity, and self-worth from market demand. If AI collapses the market for human cognition, the economic displacement might be less damaging than the identity crisis. People can survive being poor. It's harder to survive feeling useless.

Cartik Hosanagar has written about how AI dependency is already deskilling professionals — how people who use AI for writing, analysis, and decision-making find their own abilities atrophying within months. The competence inversion isn't just about who gets hired. It's about who you become when the things you used to do well are done for you. The loss isn't just economic. It's cognitive. It's personal. It's a quiet erosion of the experience of being capable.

---

I don't have a tidy resolution for this. I think anyone who does is selling something.

What I do think is that this is the most actionable of the worlds I see coming, because the intervention point is clear even if the intervention itself is hard. We know the pipeline is breaking — junior roles disappearing means senior expertise stops being created. We know the education system is misaligned — training for compliance in a world that rewards originality. We know the people who were marginalised for thinking differently may be the ones who show us what human cognition is actually for, once the routine work is done by machines.

The fork in the road is real, and I think we're already on it. One path leads to augmentation: help people discover and develop the cognitive capabilities that are genuinely, durably human. The other path leads somewhere less comfortable but perhaps more honest: help people build lives of meaning and dignity in a world that may not need their labour.

I suspect the right answer involves both paths simultaneously. I also suspect that most of us, myself included, are not yet ready to take the second one seriously. It requires letting go of the idea that your worth is what you produce — and that's a harder problem than any technical one I've ever encountered.
