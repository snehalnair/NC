# The Stratification

Last month I watched a friend negotiate a commercial lease. She is smart, experienced, runs a profitable business. Her landlord's team showed up with an AI-drafted proposal that had modelled her revenue from public filings, estimated her switching costs from Google Maps foot-traffic data, and benchmarked comparable rents in three cities she'd never heard of. She had a PDF from her accountant and a gut feeling about the neighbourhood.

She signed at twelve percent above market.

She doesn't know that yet. She will, eventually, when someone with better tools tells her. But by then the gap will be somewhere else too — in the investments she chose, the insurance she accepted, the school she picked for her kids based on rankings that someone else's AI had already gamed. Each individual decision looks fine. The accumulation is a different life.

I keep thinking about that lease. Not because it's dramatic — it isn't. Because it's ordinary. And because the gap between having a system that synthesises every relevant variable and not having one is going to compound in a way we don't have good intuitions for.

---

## The math of compounding advantage

Here's a claim I want to make carefully, because it's the kind of thing that sounds alarmist until you sit with the arithmetic.

If someone with AI-augmented cognition makes decisions that are thirty percent better — not thirty percent more, but thirty percent closer to optimal across health, finance, career, relationships — and that advantage compounds daily, then after five years you are not looking at two people in the same economic species. You are looking at divergence that makes the current wealth gap seem like rounding error.

Thirty percent is probably too high as a daily figure. But it's probably too low as a description of the gap between someone paying two hundred dollars a month for frontier AI access and someone interacting with a free ad-supported model that's three generations behind. The person paying for the frontier model is already making measurably better investment decisions, writing more persuasive proposals, catching diagnostic signals their doctor missed, structuring their children's education around personalised cognitive profiles. The gap is small right now. It is small the way a riverbed crack is small before the spring thaw.

Previous technology waves democratised access. The telephone gave you the ability to talk to someone far away. The internet gave you the ability to find information. The smartphone gave you both, everywhere, cheaply. But the thing being democratised was always access — to communication, to knowledge, to markets. The thing itself, the capability to use that access well, stayed with the person.

AI is different. AI democratises capability. And capability, unlike access, compounds.

When everyone got a calculator, the mathematician was still ahead. When everyone got a search engine, the researcher who knew which questions to ask still outperformed. When everyone gets an AI assistant, the person who knows how to direct it, prompt it, verify its outputs, chain its reasoning, and integrate its insights into decisions that require judgment under uncertainty — that person pulls away. Not by a fixed distance but by an accelerating one.

## Who actually has the advantage

Dario Amodei has written thoughtfully about the economic implications of advanced AI, projecting GDP growth of ten to twenty percent but acknowledging that distribution is the hard problem. He's noted that personal fortunes could reach into the trillions — not as hyperbole but as straightforward extrapolation from what happens when the returns to cognitive capital accelerate faster than any historical precedent. His proposed solution is aggressive progressive taxation and redistribution. I respect the honesty. I'm not sure it's sufficient.

The problem with redistribution as the primary answer is timing. Tax policy operates on electoral cycles. AI capability compounds on release cycles. By the time a tax framework is debated, passed, challenged, and implemented, the people it's meant to restrain have used three generations of AI to restructure their holdings in ways the framework doesn't anticipate. This isn't conspiracy. It's optimisation. The same capability that creates the wealth makes the wealth harder to redistribute. That's the structural trap.

Sam Altman has articulated a more ambitious vision — that by the mid-2030s, anyone on Earth should be able to marshal intellectual capacity equivalent to what the best-resourced person has today. A compute budget for every human. It's a genuinely generous idea. I want it to work. But I notice something about how it's framed: equivalent to what the best-resourced person has *today*. Not equivalent to what the best-resourced person has *then*. If the frontier moves faster than the floor rises, the absolute improvement is real and the relative gap widens. You've given everyone a better life. You've also given the top tier a different one entirely.

This is the thing that makes AI stratification unlike the stratification that came before. The phone gap was binary — you either had one or you didn't. Once you had one, the advantage was roughly equal. The AI capability gap is continuous and self-reinforcing. There isn't a point where you "have AI" the way you "have a phone." There's a spectrum: from ad-supported chatbots that hallucinate and push engagement, to personal AI systems fine-tuned on your cognitive patterns, your decision history, your goals, your specific weaknesses and how to compensate for them. Between those poles is not a gap. It's a gradient that determines, with increasing precision, what kind of life you live.

## The micro-targeting parallel

Consider what happened with social media. Facebook and TikTok gave everyone access to a platform. The real product — the one that generated the value — was not the platform. It was the ability to micro-target users based on their behavioural data. The people who had access to the targeting tools experienced a fundamentally different technology than the people being targeted. Same app. Different realities.

Kate Crawford and Shoshana Zuboff have spent years documenting this dynamic. Crawford's argument that AI functions as extraction technology — pulling value from human experience as raw material — was prescient when she made it and is becoming more obviously correct. Zuboff's framework of surveillance capitalism, where human behaviour is claimed as free raw material for prediction and modification, describes not a bug in the system but its business model.

In the stratification scenario, this dynamic scales vertically. The five percent don't just micro-target the ninety-five percent's purchasing decisions. Their AI systems micro-optimise the ninety-five percent's behaviour across every domain — what jobs they're offered, what credit they qualify for, what information they see, what options they believe they have. Not through coercion. Through optimisation so pervasive it becomes invisible. Nobody oppresses anyone. The AI systems serving owners you'll never meet simply make your choices for you, one nudge at a time, each nudge individually reasonable, the accumulation a life you didn't choose.

Kai-Fu Lee has pointed out that this stratification operates between nations as well as within them. US and Chinese AI investment patterns are creating a bipolar technology landscape where countries without frontier AI capacity become, in effect, cognitive colonies — their populations interacting with AI systems built elsewhere, optimised for someone else's interests, with no meaningful ability to build alternatives. The cognitive gap between a subsistence farmer in sub-Saharan Africa and a hedge fund manager in Greenwich is already civilisational. AI doesn't just preserve that gap. It turns it into a different kind of distance entirely.

## The neurodivergence exception

Alex Karp has made an argument I find uncomfortable and possibly important. He's claimed that two groups will thrive in an AI-dominated economy: people who work with their hands in ways robots can't replicate, and neurodivergent thinkers whose cognition doesn't follow the patterns AI was trained on. He credits his own dyslexia for much of what made Palantir successful — a way of seeing problems that couldn't be automated because it was never formalised. In 2026, Palantir launched a fellowship programme specifically for neurodivergent founders, which suggests this isn't just rhetoric.

There's something in this that rings true. If AI automates the median — the average way of thinking, the standard approach to problems, the conventional pattern of reasoning — then people who never thought that way have a structural advantage. Their cognition is pre-adapted for a world where the median has been commoditised.

But there's something Karp's thesis leaves out, and it matters enormously. Most neurodivergent people have been told their entire lives that their difference is a deficit. Not an advantage. Not a different way of processing. A problem to be fixed. Decades of educational systems, social expectations, and clinical frameworks have been devoted to pushing neurodivergent cognition toward the median — the same median that AI is about to commoditise.

The cruel irony is this: the very cognitive patterns that might be most valuable in an AI-saturated economy have been systematically suppressed by the institutions that were supposed to help. If Karp is right that neurodivergent thinking is the AI-era advantage, then the advantage is only accessible to neurodivergent people who know what their specific advantage is — who have managed, despite everything, to understand their own cognition well enough to deploy it deliberately. That's a small fraction of an already small population.

A 2025 paper on what the authors called cognitive castes argued that AI is creating new epistemic class divisions — stratification not by wealth or education but by the ability to use AI effectively, which correlates with but is not identical to both. The neurodivergent population sits oddly in this framework. Some will leap to the top of the new hierarchy, their pattern-breaking cognition suddenly legible as genius rather than disorder. Many more will be pushed further to the margins, their differences amplified by AI systems designed for neurotypical interaction patterns, their potential advantage invisible to themselves and everyone around them.

## The self-critique

I should be honest about the problems in my own argument.

The five percent versus ninety-five percent framing is rhetorically useful and empirically sloppy. Reality is a gradient, not a binary. There's a continuous distribution of AI capability access, and the boundaries between tiers are fuzzy, shifting, and different across domains. Someone might have frontier AI for their work and free-tier AI for their health decisions. The clean split makes for good writing and bad analysis.

The democratisation argument isn't wrong, just slow. Smartphones reached the bottom billion in roughly fifteen years. It's plausible that frontier AI capability reaches most of the world's population within a similar timeframe. If the compounding advantage story is right, fifteen years is long enough for the gap to become civilisational. If it's wrong — if AI capability turns out to have diminishing returns, or if the gap matters less than I think — then the democratisation timeline might be fast enough. I don't know which story is correct. I'm arguing the scary one because I think it deserves more attention than it's getting.

And then there's the hardest question, the one I genuinely don't know how to answer. If the top tier is genuinely cognitively superhuman after years of compounded AI amplification — if they really are making better decisions, creating more value, solving harder problems — is the resulting stratification injustice or meritocracy with better tools? We don't have a moral framework for a world where cognitive inequality is chosen and productive. We have frameworks for oppression, for discrimination, for unearned privilege. We don't have one for a world where the privilege is earned, in the sense that the person did the work of learning to use the tool well, but the tool was only available because they could afford it. That's a new kind of unfairness and I don't think our existing political vocabulary can name it yet.

## What I keep coming back to

I build technology that's meant to help people understand each other — to close the gap between what someone means and what someone hears. The honest version of my position is this: if tools like the ones I'm building reach the ninety-five percent, that's good. But the five percent will use them too. The curve shifts upward while preserving the distribution. Like giving everyone a calculator: everyone is better off, and the mathematician is still ahead.

The even more honest version: if I build a premium product, my early adopters will be tech-literate, educated, English-speaking people who are already on the favourable side of the distribution. I will be part of the stratification before I have any chance of being part of the solution. That's not a comfortable thing to sit with. It's the thing that's true.

I don't have a clean ending for this. The people writing optimistic scenarios about AI democratisation are not wrong about the direction. They may be wrong about the speed. And in the gap between direction and speed — in the decade or two where the frontier is pulling away faster than the floor is rising — real lives are being shaped by asymmetries that feel small now and won't feel small later.

My friend signed that lease eight weeks ago. She's happy with it. She doesn't know what she doesn't know. That's the mechanism. Not dramatic dispossession. Quiet divergence. The slow, invisible process by which people with better cognitive tools drift into a different world, one optimised decision at a time, until the distance between the worlds is too large for anyone standing in the other one to see across.

That's what keeps me up at night. Not the dystopia. The plausibility.
