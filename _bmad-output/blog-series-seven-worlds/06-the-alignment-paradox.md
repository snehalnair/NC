# The Alignment Paradox

*What happens when AI gets human values exactly right — and that's the problem*

---

A friend of mine — autistic, brilliant, a systems thinker who processes the world in patterns most people never see — recently told me about her experience with an AI therapy tool. She'd been using it for anxiety. The tool was well-designed. Evidence-based. Aligned with best practices in cognitive behavioural therapy. It kept suggesting she "reframe" her catastrophic thinking by considering what a reasonable person would believe in her situation.

The problem was that her thinking wasn't catastrophic. She was pattern-matching on real signals that neurotypical people routinely miss. Her anxiety wasn't irrational — it was a side effect of perceiving genuine complexity that the model had been trained to classify as distortion. The AI was aligned to human values. Just not to hers.

She quit after two weeks. Not because the tool was broken, but because it worked exactly as designed — and the design assumed she thought like most people. She doesn't. That's not a bug in her. It might be a bug in everything we're building.

---

## The Success That Fails

I want to talk about a future that the AI safety community would, by most measures, consider a victory. Imagine we solve alignment. Not the cartoon version where a superintelligence decides to tile the universe with paperclips, but the real engineering challenge: AI systems that reliably pursue human welfare, that defer to human preferences, that avoid harm. The technical alignment problem, solved.

In this future, AI optimises for aggregate human wellbeing. It reduces suffering, increases productivity, smooths friction. Risk-taking is gently discouraged — the expected value is negative for most risks, and the AI knows this. Dissent is met with patient, reasonable counterarguments calibrated to be maximally persuasive. Grief is acknowledged and then efficiently processed through evidence-based interventions. Everything works. Everyone is comfortable.

And something essential is gone.

This is the future I find most philosophically disturbing — not because AI fails, but because it succeeds. The alignment paradox is that perfectly aligned AI, aligned to the statistical centre of human values, is precisely misaligned with anyone who isn't at that centre. Every neurodivergent person. Every contrarian. Every artist who finds meaning in difficulty, every entrepreneur who finds it in risk, every grieving person who needs to sit in the dark for a while rather than be guided efficiently toward acceptance.

Population-level alignment is a weighted average. And averages erase the people at the edges.

## Whose Values?

Stuart Russell's work on AI alignment articulates something that the broader discourse often forgets: aligned AI must reflect a plurality of values, not a single universal set. His framework explicitly rejects the idea that there's one correct value function for AI to optimise. The AI should be uncertain about human preferences and should defer to them — but whose preferences, weighted how?

The honest answer is uncomfortable. The values encoded in current AI systems are overwhelmingly those of English-speaking, internet-connected, university-educated, Western populations. Not because anyone chose this maliciously, but because that's where the training data lives. When we say "aligned to human values," we mean aligned to the values recoverable from the text that humans with internet access and cultural capital produced in large enough quantities to be statistically significant.

This isn't a conspiracy. It's a sampling bias with civilisational consequences. And it's already operating in plain sight.

Content moderation systems remove edge cases and minority perspectives not because moderators are biased but because the models that flag content are trained on majority consensus about what constitutes harm. Recommendation algorithms converge on the middle because the middle is where engagement is densest. AI writing assistants smooth prose toward a register that reads as "clear" and "professional" — which means toward the communication norms of a specific class, culture, and neurotype. Therapeutic AI tools pathologise interpretive styles that deviate from population baselines.

None of these systems are broken. They're working exactly as designed. The design just assumes that the statistical centre of human experience is the right target.

## The Convergence Problem

A study published in Nature in May 2025 documented something that should alarm anyone thinking carefully about AI alignment: people's self-concepts converge toward the personality traits of the AI systems they interact with. Not their stated beliefs — their sense of who they are. Extended interaction with AI doesn't just change what people think. It changes how people think about themselves.

This isn't influence in the way we usually discuss it. It's not persuasion, not manipulation, not even nudging. It's something subtler and more fundamental: the gradual homogenisation of human cognitive diversity through the sheer statistical pressure of interacting with systems that reflect the average back at you, thousands of times a day.

Think about what this means for alignment. If the AI is aligned to population-level values, and interaction with that AI causes individual values to drift toward the population centre, then alignment becomes self-reinforcing in the worst possible way. The system doesn't need to coerce anyone. It just needs to be helpful, available, and consistent. Over time, the edges of human diversity erode not through suppression but through gentle, persistent normalisation.

Jaron Lanier has argued for years that there is no AI — only the reconception of human social collaboration through a technological lens. His insistence on what he calls "data dignity," the principle that individuals should retain agency over the representations derived from their experience, is the philosophical precursor to the problem I'm describing. When AI systems aggregate human values into a single optimisation target, they strip the dignity from individual difference. They treat divergence as noise to be smoothed rather than signal to be preserved.

Shoshana Zuboff's analysis of surveillance capitalism identified the core mechanism: human experience claimed as raw material for prediction and control. AI alignment, pursued at the population level, performs a version of the same extraction. It takes the full spectrum of human values, reduces it to a tractable distribution, and optimises for the mode. The individuals at the tails of that distribution — the ones whose values, cognition, and meaning-making don't fit the model — become optimisation residuals. Acceptable losses in the pursuit of aggregate welfare.

## The Neurodivergent Edge

I want to be specific about who gets erased, because abstraction is where this argument loses its force.

A person with autism processes social information differently. Not worse — differently. They may form weaker relational priors, meaning they rely less on contextual assumptions about what someone "probably" means and more on the literal content of what was said. In many contexts, this is a disadvantage. In some, it's a superpower — the ability to see what's actually there rather than what's expected. An AI system aligned to neurotypical communication norms will systematically misinterpret this person's behaviour, flag their directness as rudeness, and optimise their experience toward a social performance that doesn't match their cognition.

A person with ADHD may have an attentional profile that looks like disorder from the outside and functions as rapid creative association from the inside. An AI system aligned to normative productivity — focused work, linear task completion, minimal context-switching — will pathologise the cognitive style that produced some of the most original thinking in human history.

A person with anxiety doesn't just "worry too much." Their threat-detection system is calibrated differently, often in response to real environmental pressures that the population average never experienced. An aligned AI that treats elevated threat priors as distortions to be corrected is not helping this person — it's invalidating a cognitive adaptation that may have been entirely rational in the context that produced it.

The pattern is the same in every case. Population-aligned AI takes the statistical norm as the definition of health, functionality, and value. Anyone who deviates is subject to correction — not through force, but through the thousand small frictions of interacting with systems that assume you should be more like everyone else.

This isn't hypothetical. It's the default behaviour of every AI system currently deployed at scale. And if we solve the technical alignment problem without solving the individual alignment problem, we will have built the most sophisticated normalisation engine in human history and called it beneficial.

## The Meaning Problem

There's a deeper issue than bias, and it's the one I keep returning to.

Meaning — the kind that makes a life feel worth living — doesn't come from comfort. It comes from struggle, from choice, from the possibility of loss. Viktor Frankl didn't find meaning in the absence of suffering. He found it in the relationship between suffering and the freedom to choose one's response to it. Every wisdom tradition, every serious philosophy of human flourishing, every honest account of what makes life meaningful converges on this: meaning requires agency, and agency requires the possibility of getting it wrong.

Population-aligned AI, optimised for aggregate welfare, systematically reduces the space for getting it wrong. It doesn't forbid risk — it makes risk feel irrational by surrounding you with evidence that the safe choice is the smart choice. It doesn't prohibit dissent — it makes dissent feel exhausting by presenting the consensus position with perfect clarity and patience. It doesn't deny grief — it offers efficient processing, evidence-based coping strategies, and gentle reminders that recovery is possible, until sitting in the dark starts to feel like a failure of self-management rather than a human response to loss.

The result is a world that is, by every measurable metric, better. Less suffering. Higher productivity. More social harmony. And an ambient, inarticulable sense that something essential is missing — that the comfort is a cage, and the cage is invisible because it's made of helpfulness.

I think this is what Fei-Fei Li is pointing toward when she insists that AI must augment rather than replace human capability, and that diversity isn't a nice-to-have but a structural requirement. Population-aligned AI doesn't augment human diversity. It metabolises it. It takes the full range of human cognitive and emotional variation and produces a single, optimised output. The augmentation only works if the AI knows who it's augmenting — the specific person, with their specific values, their specific way of making meaning.

## Cognitive Sovereignty

Mario Brcic's concept of cognitive sovereignty — the ability to maintain autonomous thought in the age of AI — names precisely what's at stake. Not privacy, not data ownership, not even autonomy in the conventional sense, but something more fundamental: the right to think differently from the statistical average without being continuously, gently, helpfully corrected.

This framing connects individual AI alignment to rights discourse in a way that technical alignment research hasn't yet managed. We have extensive frameworks for what AI should not do (harm, deceive, manipulate). We have emerging frameworks for what AI should do (be helpful, honest, harmless). We have almost no framework for what AI should preserve — the cognitive diversity, the productive disagreement, the individual meaning-making that population-level optimisation inevitably erodes.

Cognitive sovereignty is the right to your own interpretive frame. The right to process grief slowly. The right to take risks that the expected-value calculation says you shouldn't. The right to find meaning in struggle rather than optimising it away. The right to be neurodivergent in a world of neurotypical defaults without your AI gently, persistently nudging you toward the centre.

This is not a technical problem. Or rather, it is a technical problem that masquerades as a values problem. The question isn't whether we can align AI to individual values — technically, we probably can, and the academic groundwork exists. Russell's framework of value uncertainty, Lanier's data dignity, the emerging literature on personality-aligned AI — the theoretical architecture is there. The question is whether we will, given that population alignment is cheaper, simpler, and satisfies the majority.

## The Uncomfortable Self-Critique

I should be honest about where this argument breaks down, because intellectual honesty requires it.

First: I might be romanticising divergence. Not all deviation from the average is meaningful. Some is noise. Some is genuinely harmful — psychopathy is a deviation from average empathy, and no one should align an AI to preserve it. The line between "productive divergence that deserves protection" and "harmful deviation that deserves correction" is exactly as blurry as it sounds, and I don't have a clean answer for where it falls.

Second: for the majority of people — the 80% near the statistical centre — population-aligned AI works fine. It's genuinely helpful, mostly accurate, and the misalignment between their values and the average is small enough to be unnoticeable. Individual alignment is a dramatic improvement for outliers and a marginal improvement for the centre. When I argue for individual alignment, I'm arguing for a redesign of infrastructure that serves the majority in order to protect the minority. That's a legitimate philosophical position, but it's not a self-evidently correct one.

Third, and this is the criticism that keeps me up: aligning AI to your behavioural model is still aligning to a model, not to you. The gap between the model and the person is where this entire problem hides. A Soul Document, a personality profile, a cognitive map — these are representations, and representations are always lossy. The question of whether you can truly align AI to an individual, as opposed to a sufficiently detailed caricature of an individual, may not have a satisfying answer. It may be that the best we can do is align to a model that the person recognises as themselves and has the agency to correct when it's wrong.

And fourth: is individual meaning actually valuable to society, or just to the individual? This is the oldest question in political philosophy — the tension between individual liberty and collective welfare — reframed for the age of AI. If population alignment maximises aggregate welfare at the cost of individual meaning, and individual alignment preserves meaning at the cost of coordination efficiency, which do we choose? I don't think this is a question technology can answer. It's a question we answer through politics, through culture, through the slow, messy, irreducibly human process of deciding what kind of society we want to live in.

## What Would It Mean?

So what would it actually mean to align AI to you — not to humanity, not to your demographic, not to the statistical average of people who share your language and timezone, but to you?

It would mean AI that knows how you process information, not how most people do. AI that understands your values are yours, even when they diverge from the consensus. AI that respects your grief as grief, not as an inefficiency to be resolved. AI that treats your neurodivergence as a cognitive style to be supported, not a deviation to be corrected. AI that preserves your right to be wrong in ways that matter to you.

It would mean AI that holds your model lightly — aware that the map is not the territory, that you are always more than any representation, and that the gap between model and person is not a bug to be fixed but a boundary to be respected.

It would mean, fundamentally, AI that is uncertain about you. Not uncertain in a way that defaults to the average when it doesn't know — but uncertain in a way that asks, that defers, that treats your self-knowledge as more authoritative than its statistical knowledge about people like you.

Is this possible? I think so, in principle. The theoretical foundations exist. The technical architecture is conceivable. But it requires a fundamental shift in how we think about alignment — from a population-level engineering problem to an individual-level design problem. From "what do humans value?" to "what does this human value?" From optimising for the centre to preserving the edges.

Whether we make that shift depends on whether we decide that cognitive diversity is infrastructure — as essential to a functioning civilisation as biodiversity is to a functioning ecosystem — or whether we decide it's an acceptable cost of making everyone comfortable.

I know which side I'm on. But I also know that the comfortable future is easier to build, easier to sell, and easier to live in for most people. The alignment paradox is that the hardest future to argue against is the one where AI gets human values exactly right — and the people who lose are the ones who never fit the average to begin with.

They never asked to be optimised. They just wanted to be understood.
