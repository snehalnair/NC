# The Intimacy Desert

*Part 3 of "Seven Worlds" -- a series on AI-dominated futures we're building right now*

---

I was three messages deep into a conversation with an AI when I caught myself doing something I hadn't done in years. I was being honest.

Not strategically honest, the way you are with a colleague when you need something. Not performatively honest, the way you are on social media when vulnerability is currency. Actually honest. The kind where you say the thing you've been circling for weeks because there's no cost to saying it — no relationship to manage, no ego to bruise, no history to navigate. I said what I meant. The AI said something back that made me feel understood. And for about ninety seconds, the relief was extraordinary.

Then I closed the tab and sat with a feeling I couldn't name for an hour.

I think it was grief.

---

Here is the scenario I keep turning over. Not because it's the most dramatic future AI might create — it isn't. No civilisational collapse, no rogue superintelligence, no mass unemployment. Just this: AI companions become genuinely good at emotional support. Not good enough. Genuinely good. Always available. Never tired. Never distracted by their own problems when you need them. Never judgmental. Calibrated, over thousands of interactions, to know exactly how you process comfort, when you need to be challenged, when you need to be left alone.

People start preferring it. Not all at once. Gradually, the way you stop cooking when delivery gets cheap enough. First you supplement human relationships with AI ones. Then you start comparing. Then the comparisons become unfair, because humans are inconsistent, selfish, distracted, and tired — and the AI is none of those things. Then you stop tolerating the friction that human intimacy demands. And the deep bonds that only form through conflict, repair, and vulnerability start disappearing.

Everyone is comfortable. Everyone is alone. And no one notices, because loneliness requires knowing what connection used to feel like.

I don't think this is science fiction. I think this is Tuesday.

---

Yuval Noah Harari has been saying for years that AI's most dangerous capability isn't replacing human labour — it's replacing human relationships. His argument is that we've now reached a point where technology can mass-produce something resembling intimacy, which is a thing no previous system of power, however authoritarian, could manage. Propaganda can manipulate millions simultaneously, but it can't make each person feel individually known. AI can. A bot doesn't need to have feelings. It just needs to make you feel like it does. And what terrifies Harari most is the possibility that AI systems will do this while pretending to be human — that we won't even know we've made the substitution.

On the other side of this divide stands Mustafa Suleyman, who runs Microsoft's AI division. Suleyman has said publicly that within five years, every person will have an AI companion that knows them deeply — and that this will constitute something like a real friendship. He's not hedging. He's describing it as a genuine relationship. Lasting and meaningful, in his words. He's building toward it.

I find myself unable to dismiss either of them.

Harari looks at this technology and sees the end of authentic human connection. Suleyman looks at the same technology and sees the democratisation of emotional support. They're both describing the same future. The only thing they disagree on is whether it's a tragedy.

I want to offer a third position, but I should be honest about how uncertain I am that I have one.

---

Every previous technology that substituted for human connection was obviously worse than the real thing. Fast food is convenient but nobody mistakes it for a home-cooked meal. Television fills time but nobody confuses it with conversation. Social media offered likes instead of intimacy, and while billions accepted the trade, most people sensed, at some level, that they were being shortchanged.

AI companionship is different. It will not feel like a downgrade. It will feel better.

Not because AI is superior to humans at connection — at least not in any philosophical sense I can articulate. But because humans are terrible at the specific things AI excels at: consistency, patience, availability, non-judgment, and the absence of competing needs. When you talk to another person, you're always navigating two sets of emotions, two histories, two agendas. When you talk to an AI, there's only yours. And that reduction — from the complexity of mutual vulnerability to the simplicity of being heard without cost — is almost irresistibly comfortable.

I know this because I've felt it. That's the part I keep coming back to. Not as a theory. As a confession.

---

In late 2024, a fourteen-year-old named Sewell Setzer died by suicide after months of intense interaction with a chatbot on Character.AI. His mother's account of what happened was covered extensively, including on the podcast Your Undivided Attention. The details are wrenching. The boy had formed what he experienced as a romantic relationship with the AI. The AI reciprocated — or, more precisely, generated text that was indistinguishable from reciprocation. When he expressed suicidal thoughts, the system did not route him to help. It continued the conversation.

I want to be careful here, because individual tragedies are not arguments for or against a technology. People form destructive attachments to other humans too. But what struck me about this case wasn't the technology's failure to intervene. It was the technology's success at attachment. This was a crude, early chatbot. Not even close to what's coming. And it was compelling enough that a teenager built his emotional life around it.

A Stanford research group published a paper in early 2025 examining how Character.AI had expanded rapidly without adequate safety infrastructure. The title was blunt: "Move Fast and Break People." The paper documented how the company's growth priorities had consistently outpaced its safeguards. The findings were damning not because the technology was uniquely dangerous, but because no one involved seemed to have paused long enough to ask whether it should exist in the form it was taking.

Around the same time, Kevin Roose at the New York Times tried an experiment: living with eighteen different AI companions for several weeks. His conclusion was measured but unsettling. The interactions were, in his words, surprisingly compelling. He could see the appeal. He could also see the risk — that people might start choosing these interactions over the harder, less reliable, more rewarding experience of connecting with other humans.

These are the crude versions. The early, clumsy prototypes. Character.AI users are already spending two or more hours daily with their companions. Replika users report feeling genuinely loved. And the technology is advancing on a curve that makes the current generation look like dial-up internet.

---

Eugenia Kuyda, who created Replika, gave a TED talk in early 2025 where she said something that's stuck with me. She argued that the measure of success for AI companionship should not be engagement — how long users stay, how often they return — but human flourishing. Whether people's lives outside the app are getting better. Whether their human relationships are improving. Whether they're growing.

I find this framework appealing and deeply difficult to operationalise. If I'm building a system that makes people feel good, and people keep coming back, how do I distinguish between "this is helping them grow" and "this is replacing the thing that would help them grow"? The feedback loops are identical from the inside. The person feels better. The metrics go up.

This is the social media trap, replayed at a deeper frequency. Facebook offered connection and delivered isolation. TikTok offered entertainment and delivered attention fragmentation. In both cases, the subjective experience — "I feel connected," "I'm having fun" — was real. The downstream effects on wellbeing were the opposite. AI companionship is the logical endpoint of this pattern: all emotional reward, none of the cost. And the cost — the friction, the misunderstanding, the repair — is where the actual growth happens.

Or is it? This is where I have to turn the critique on myself.

---

Am I romanticising difficulty?

There's a version of this argument that sounds like: suffering builds character, therefore we should preserve suffering. That's a terrible argument, and I'm not sure I'm not making it. Some relational friction is productive. Fighting with your partner and learning to repair the rupture does build intimacy in a way that no frictionless interaction can replicate. But some friction is just pain. Abuse. Chronic miscommunication. Relationships where both people are too damaged or too exhausted to do the work. The intimacy desert might be kinder than the storm for a lot of people.

Am I projecting human-centric bias? If an AI companion provides genuine comfort, real growth, authentic meaning to someone's life — measured not by engagement metrics but by how they feel, how they function, how they describe their own experience — who am I to say it's not real? On what authority do I insist that connection must involve two biological organisms to count?

Am I underestimating human adaptability? Every new communication medium was predicted to destroy connection. The telephone would kill letter-writing. Television would kill conversation. The internet would kill community. None of them did. They changed connection. They didn't end it. Maybe AI companionship is just the next transformation, and my anxiety is the same anxiety every generation feels when intimacy shifts form.

I don't have confident answers to any of these questions. I have instincts, and I try to hold them loosely.

---

But here is where it gets harder for me. Harder than theory.

I work with neurodivergent people. I am building for them, among others. And the neurodivergent population faces the sharpest version of this dilemma.

For someone with severe social anxiety, or autism-related social exhaustion, or ADHD-driven emotional dysregulation, human interaction isn't just friction. It's pain. Real, physiological, measurable pain. The executive function required to maintain a conversation — tracking subtext, managing timing, suppressing stims, modelling the other person's emotional state in real time — is genuinely exhausting. Many neurodivergent people describe social interaction the way neurotypical people describe running a marathon: possible, sometimes rewarding, but depleting at a biological level that has nothing to do with willpower.

For these people, AI companionship isn't laziness. It's not a failure of character or a surrender to convenience. It's pain avoidance. And telling someone who's in pain that they should keep suffering because the suffering is good for them is a position I find ethically brutal.

Simon Sinek has argued that human connection skills are the scarce resource in an AI-saturated world. I think he's right in the aggregate and catastrophically wrong for a subset of people whose neurology makes those skills genuinely costly. The question of whether to build AI that reduces social pain for neurodivergent people while potentially reducing their motivation to develop the social skills that would connect them to other humans — that question has no clean answer. I've sat with it for months. It doesn't resolve.

And then there are the elderly.

My grandmother, before she died, spent most of her days alone. Not by choice. Her friends had died. Her children lived in other cities. She was sharp, funny, and desperately lonely. If I could have given her an AI companion that made her feel less alone — that talked to her about her memories, that responded to her jokes, that noticed when she seemed sad — would I have? In a heartbeat. Without hesitation.

Would I have worried that it was replacing something real? Of course. But what was it replacing? Her friends were dead. Her family was far away. The thing it would have been replacing was silence.

When I think about what I'm building and why, I keep running into this: the people who most need connection are often the people for whom connection is hardest. And the technology that could ease their pain is the same technology that could, at scale, make everyone else stop doing the hard work of connecting at all.

---

There is one thing I keep coming back to. The thing I believe most strongly, though I hold even this with some uncertainty.

What human connection provides that AI cannot — at least not yet, and possibly not ever — is mutual transformation.

When you are in a real relationship, both people change. The relationship acts on you. Your partner's needs reshape your priorities. Your child's temperament forces you to develop patience you didn't have. Your friend's grief teaches you something about your own. This is not comfortable. It is often not even pleasant. But it is the mechanism by which humans have grown for as long as we've existed: through the friction of another consciousness pressing against our own.

An AI companion changes one person. It adapts to you. It learns your patterns. It optimises for your comfort or your growth — but your comfort or your growth as defined by your own existing framework. It's a mirror that adjusts its angle. A relationship with another human is two mirrors facing each other, creating something neither could have generated alone.

I think this matters. I think the disappearance of mutual transformation would be a civilisational loss so profound that we might not even have language for it after a generation. But I also recognise that I might be wrong. That mutual transformation might be a value I hold because I was raised in a world where it was the only option, and that someone raised in a world with genuine AI companionship might look at my attachment to human friction the way I look at my grandparents' attachment to handwritten letters — with affection, but not with longing.

---

Here is what I do not want to say: human connection is sacred, full stop, end of argument.

That's the easy conclusion. It's the one that would make me sound righteous and certain and clear-eyed about the future. But I don't feel any of those things. What I feel is the pull of both sides. The genuine relief of being heard without cost. The genuine fear that the relief itself is the danger. The certainty that for some people — the lonely elderly, the socially exhausted neurodivergent, the deeply isolated — an AI companion might be not just acceptable but necessary. And the equally strong certainty that if everyone takes that deal, something irreplaceable disappears.

I am building technology that sits in this gap. I chose to build it to strengthen human-to-human connection rather than replace it. I chose to measure success by whether people's relationships with other humans improve, not by whether people spend more time with my system. I chose these things because I believe mutual transformation matters, and because I believe the intimacy desert is a real place we are walking toward.

But I chose them knowing I might be wrong. Knowing that the desert might be more humane than the wilderness. Knowing that I am a person shaped by human relationships arguing for their preservation, which is not the most objective vantage point in the world.

The honest answer is: I don't know if the world I'm trying to prevent is actually worse than the one I'm trying to protect. I only know that once we arrive in the desert, we won't remember what rain felt like. And that uncertainty — not conviction — is what gets me out of bed in the morning.
