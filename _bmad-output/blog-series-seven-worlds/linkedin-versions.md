## WORLD 1: THE DELEGATION COLLAPSE
================================================================================

### Suggested Title
**The Last Skill You Lose Is the One You Don't Notice Losing**

---

### LinkedIn Post Teaser
(~1,350 characters)

I drove to a restaurant last week without GPS. I'd been there a dozen times. I missed two turns and ended up lost five minutes from my own house.

That should be funny. It's also a warning.

We're running a civilisation-scale experiment in cognitive delegation -- handing over writing, analysis, decision-making, scheduling, even emotional communication to AI -- and nobody designed the experiment. There are no controls. No measurement. No plan for what happens if the results are bad.

Every previous tool replaced ONE cognitive skill. Calculators replaced arithmetic. GPS replaced navigation. Each sat in one lane.

AI sits in every lane at once. That's not the same trade.

An MIT Media Lab study tracked people using ChatGPT for writing over four months. Some measures showed up to a 55% drop in neural connectivity. The researchers called it "cognitive debt" -- effort saved now, capacity lost later.

Here's what makes this unlike any dependency we've seen: it makes you BETTER at everything while quietly eroding your ability to do those things without it.

A drug that makes you worse makes you want to quit. A tool that makes you better while hollowing you out? You'll never put it down.

I'm building in this space. I don't pretend to have solved this. But I think the least we owe ourselves is to name what's happening before the neural pathways close and the question becomes academic.

What's the last skill you noticed you'd lost to a tool?

#AI #CognitiveSovereignty #FutureOfWork #Neurodiversity #HumanAI

---

### LinkedIn Article Body

I drove to a restaurant last week without GPS. I hadn't done that in years. Not because the restaurant was new -- I'd been there a dozen times. But somewhere between the third and fourth visit, I stopped encoding the route. I just followed the blue line.

When I finally tried to get there from memory, I missed two turns and ended up on a street I didn't recognise, five minutes from my own house.

This is the kind of moment that makes you laugh at yourself. It's also the kind of moment that should terrify you.

I've been thinking about what happens when we stop doing things for ourselves -- not because we can't, but because we don't have to. Not one thing, the way calculators replaced mental arithmetic. Everything. All at once.

**The substitution that's different this time**

We've delegated cognitive tasks to tools before. Plato worried that writing would destroy memory. He had a point -- oral cultures had prodigious recall that literate cultures lost. But we gained externalized, transmissible knowledge. The trade was worth it.

Calculators replaced mental arithmetic. GPS replaced wayfinding. Spell-check replaced spelling. In each case, we lost a narrow skill and gained efficiency.

Here's what's different now: every one of those was a single substitution. Each tool sat in one cognitive lane. What's happening with AI is that the substitution is general-purpose. It spans writing, analysis, decision-making, scheduling, emotional communication, creative work, strategic thinking -- essentially the full spectrum of what we mean when we say "thinking."

[TAG: Geoffrey Hinton] has made this distinction sharply. Previous industrial revolutions automated physical labour. This one automates thinking itself. And unlike a calculator, which sits inert until you use it, these systems are increasingly agentic. They anticipate, suggest, draft, decide.

Losing mental arithmetic is survivable because everything else stays intact. Losing capacity across the entire cognitive spectrum simultaneously is a different kind of event.

**The neuroplasticity problem**

When we stopped walking everywhere and started driving, our legs didn't wither. Physical delegation has a biological floor. Your muscles atrophy without use, but basic locomotion is so deeply wired that it persists.

Cognition may not have that floor.

Neuroplasticity is usually invoked as good news. Your brain adapts! It grows! But neuroplasticity is agnostic about direction. The same mechanism that lets a London taxi driver develop an enlarged hippocampus from years of navigation also lets that structure shrink when navigation is no longer required. Use it or lose it isn't a slogan. It's a description of how neural tissue works.

An MIT Media Lab study tracked 54 participants over four months as they used ChatGPT for essay writing. The group using AI showed dramatically reduced neural connectivity -- some measures indicated up to a 55% drop. The researchers coined a term: cognitive debt. Mental effort saved in the short term generates long-term costs -- diminished critical thinking, reduced creativity, shallow information processing.

I find this sobering, though the sample was small and the paper hasn't been peer-reviewed yet. But it aligns with what Xu and colleagues formalised in a March 2026 paper that deserves wider attention. They analysed over 1,200 AI-human interaction papers and identified a pattern they call "cognitive agency surrender." Their most troubling finding: a brief 2025 surge in research on protecting human autonomy was already being swamped by 2026 by research on making autonomous AI agents more capable.

The field is building better autopilots, not better pilots.

**The addiction that doesn't look like addiction**

[TAG: Tristan Harris] and [TAG: Aza Raskin] have argued that social media created a "human downgrade." Their point about AI is that it sits in humanity's cognitive blind spot: we underestimate exponential capability curves until the effects are entrenched.

They're right, but AI delegation is harder than the social media problem. Here's why.

Social media succeeded at engagement, and addiction was the consequence. You could point to the dark patterns, the dopamine loops. You could say: this is bad for you. The harm was separable from the benefit.

AI delegation isn't like that. It isn't addiction to consumption. It's addiction to ease. And it genuinely makes your life better in almost every measurable way. Your emails are more articulate. Your schedule is more optimised. Your decisions incorporate more data.

The only thing it makes worse is something you can't easily see: your own capacity to do those things without it.

This is the cruelest possible design for a dependency. A drug that makes you worse makes you want to quit. A tool that makes you better at everything while quietly eroding the substrate that lets you function independently -- that's a tool you'll never voluntarily put down.

[TAG: Dario Amodei] has projected that roughly half of entry-level white-collar jobs could be displaced within one to five years. I give him credit for the honesty. But his framing is about jobs. What I'm worried about isn't just whether people have jobs. It's whether they retain the cognitive capacity to do them.

**The children's problem**

Everything above is about atrophy -- adults losing capabilities through disuse. For children growing up immersed in AI delegation, the risk isn't atrophy. It's foreclosure. They never build the skills in the first place.

An adult developer who leans too heavily on AI code has the underlying knowledge somewhere. It can potentially be recovered. A child who never learns to reason through difficulty, who never builds the frustration tolerance that comes from working through problems -- that child hasn't lost something. They never had it.

[TAG: Simon Sinek] made a version of this argument that I found compelling. AI can do many things, but it can't struggle for you. And it's the struggle, not the output, that develops the human capacities we actually need.

**What if I'm wrong**

Maybe cognitive atrophy fears are overstated. Every previous delegation worked out. We lost oral memory and gained literacy. We lost navigation and gained global mobility. Maybe this is just the next version of that story.

The "this time it's different" argument is deployed by every generation of technology critics, and it's almost always wrong. I might be the person in 1995 warning that email would destroy human communication.

Here's the uncomfortable possibility I keep returning to: what if atrophy is actually adaptation? If AI handles cognitive tasks better than humans, then losing independent capacity might be rational. We don't mourn our inability to outrun predators. We built cars.

But it breaks down at one point: cars don't disappear. They don't go offline. They don't get acquired by companies that change their terms of service. The cognitive infrastructure AI replaces is internal. The AI infrastructure that replaces it is external, fragile, owned by someone else, and subject to incentives that may not align with yours.

**The mechanism question**

This is the question I can't stop asking: what structural mechanism -- not principle, not good intention, not mission statement, but actual mechanism -- prevents the tools we build from creating the dependency they claim to prevent?

Every AI subscription product faces the same gravitational pull: churn reduction equals dependency. A tool that makes users more capable eventually makes them confident enough to leave. A tool that substitutes for user capability makes itself indispensable.

Facebook didn't set out to create an addiction engine. They set out to connect people. The AI industry isn't setting out to create cognitive dependency. They're setting out to be helpful. And dependency may be the structural consequence of optimising for ease.

I'm building in this space myself, and I don't pretend to have solved this. The honest answer is that designing a tool that builds human capacity rather than substituting for it requires fighting the gravitational pull of every metric that measures success in software.

**What I'm left with**

We are running a civilisation-scale experiment in cognitive delegation without controls, without measurement, and without any plan for what happens if the results are bad.

Each individual decision to let AI handle one more thing is sensible. In aggregate, they may be producing something nobody chose and nobody wants.

The last skill you lose is the one you don't notice losing. By the time you notice, you need the tool to tell you what you've lost. And the tool has no incentive to answer honestly.

## WORLD 2: THE STRATIFICATION
================================================================================

### Suggested Title
**The Quiet Divergence: How AI Creates Inequality You Can't See**

---

### LinkedIn Post Teaser
(~1,400 characters)

Last month I watched a friend negotiate a commercial lease. She's smart, experienced, runs a profitable business.

Her landlord's team showed up with an AI-drafted proposal that had modelled her revenue from public filings, estimated her switching costs from Google Maps data, and benchmarked rents in three cities she'd never heard of. She had a PDF from her accountant and a gut feeling.

She signed at 12% above market. She doesn't know that yet.

This is what AI stratification looks like. Not dramatic dispossession. Quiet divergence. One optimised decision at a time.

If someone with AI-augmented cognition makes decisions that are 30% closer to optimal across health, finance, career -- and that advantage compounds daily -- after five years you're not looking at two people in the same economic species.

Previous tech democratised ACCESS. The phone gave you the ability to call someone. The internet gave you the ability to find information. The capability to USE that access well stayed with the person.

AI democratises CAPABILITY. And capability, unlike access, compounds.

The gap between someone paying $200/month for frontier AI and someone using a free ad-supported chatbot three generations behind isn't just about quality. It's about compounding life outcomes across every decision, every day, every year.

What makes you most uneasy about AI-driven inequality -- the speed of it or the invisibility of it?

#AI #FutureOfWork #Inequality #CognitiveSovereignty #HumanAI

---

### LinkedIn Article Body

Last month I watched a friend negotiate a commercial lease. She is smart, experienced, runs a profitable business. Her landlord's team showed up with an AI-drafted proposal that had modelled her revenue from public filings, estimated her switching costs from Google Maps foot-traffic data, and benchmarked comparable rents in three cities she'd never heard of.

She had a PDF from her accountant and a gut feeling about the neighbourhood.

She signed at twelve percent above market. She doesn't know that yet.

I keep thinking about that lease. Not because it's dramatic -- it isn't. Because it's ordinary. And because the gap between having a system that synthesises every relevant variable and not having one is going to compound in a way we don't have good intuitions for.

**The math of compounding advantage**

Here's a claim I want to make carefully. If someone with AI-augmented cognition makes decisions that are thirty percent closer to optimal -- across health, finance, career, relationships -- and that advantage compounds daily, then after five years you are not looking at two people in the same economic species. You are looking at divergence that makes the current wealth gap seem like rounding error.

Thirty percent is probably too high as a daily figure. But it's probably too low as a description of the gap between someone paying two hundred dollars a month for frontier AI and someone using a free ad-supported chatbot three generations behind.

The person paying for frontier AI is already making measurably better investment decisions, writing more persuasive proposals, catching diagnostic signals their doctor missed. The gap is small right now. It is small the way a riverbed crack is small before the spring thaw.

Previous technology waves democratised access. The telephone gave you the ability to talk to someone far away. The internet gave you the ability to find information. But the thing being democratised was always access. The capability to use that access well stayed with the person.

AI is different. AI democratises capability. And capability, unlike access, compounds.

When everyone got a calculator, the mathematician was still ahead. When everyone got a search engine, the researcher who knew which questions to ask still outperformed. When everyone gets an AI assistant, the person who knows how to direct it, verify its outputs, chain its reasoning, and integrate its insights into judgment under uncertainty -- that person pulls away. Not by a fixed distance. By an accelerating one.

**Who actually has the advantage**

[TAG: Dario Amodei] has projected GDP growth of ten to twenty percent but acknowledges that distribution is the hard problem. He's noted that personal fortunes could reach into the trillions. His proposed solution is aggressive progressive taxation.

The problem with redistribution as the primary answer is timing. Tax policy operates on electoral cycles. AI capability compounds on release cycles. By the time a tax framework is debated, passed, and implemented, the people it's meant to restrain have used three generations of AI to restructure their holdings in ways the framework doesn't anticipate.

[TAG: Sam Altman] has articulated a more ambitious vision -- by the mid-2030s, anyone on Earth should be able to marshal intellectual capacity equivalent to what the best-resourced person has today. It's a genuinely generous idea. But notice the framing: equivalent to what the best-resourced person has *today*. Not *then*. If the frontier moves faster than the floor rises, the absolute improvement is real and the relative gap widens.

This is what makes AI stratification unlike what came before. The phone gap was binary -- you had one or you didn't. The AI capability gap is continuous and self-reinforcing. There isn't a point where you "have AI" the way you "have a phone." There's a spectrum: from ad-supported chatbots that hallucinate, to personal AI systems fine-tuned on your cognitive patterns, your goals, your specific weaknesses. Between those poles is a gradient that determines, with increasing precision, what kind of life you live.

**The micro-targeting parallel**

Consider what happened with social media. Facebook and TikTok gave everyone access to a platform. The real product was the ability to micro-target users based on behavioural data. The people with access to the targeting tools experienced a fundamentally different technology than the people being targeted. Same app. Different realities.

[TAG: Kate Crawford] and [TAG: Shoshana Zuboff] have documented this dynamic extensively. Crawford's argument that AI functions as extraction technology was prescient when she made it and is becoming obviously correct.

In the stratification scenario, this scales vertically. The top tier doesn't just micro-target purchasing decisions. Their AI systems micro-optimise behaviour across every domain -- what jobs people are offered, what credit they qualify for, what information they see. Not through coercion. Through optimisation so pervasive it becomes invisible.

[TAG: Kai-Fu Lee] has pointed out that this operates between nations too. US and Chinese AI investment patterns are creating a landscape where countries without frontier AI capacity become cognitive colonies -- populations interacting with AI built elsewhere, optimised for someone else's interests.

**The neurodivergence exception**

[TAG: Alex Karp] has claimed that two groups will thrive: people who work with their hands in ways robots can't replicate, and neurodivergent thinkers whose cognition doesn't follow the patterns AI was trained on. He credits his dyslexia for much of what made Palantir successful. In 2026, Palantir launched a fellowship specifically for neurodivergent founders.

There's something that rings true here. If AI automates the median -- the standard approach, the conventional reasoning -- then people who never thought that way have a structural advantage.

But here's what Karp's thesis leaves out. Most neurodivergent people have been told their entire lives that their difference is a deficit. Decades of educational systems and clinical frameworks have pushed neurodivergent cognition toward the median -- the same median AI is about to commoditise.

The cruel irony: the cognitive patterns that might be most valuable have been systematically suppressed by the institutions that were supposed to help. The advantage is only accessible to neurodivergent people who have managed to understand their own cognition well enough to deploy it deliberately. That's a small fraction of an already small population.

**The honest reckoning**

The five percent versus ninety-five percent framing is rhetorically useful and empirically sloppy. Reality is a gradient, not a binary. Someone might have frontier AI for work and free-tier AI for health decisions.

The democratisation argument isn't wrong, just slow. Smartphones reached the bottom billion in roughly fifteen years. If AI compounds faster than it democratises, fifteen years is long enough for the gap to become civilisational.

And then the hardest question: if the top tier is genuinely cognitively superhuman after years of compounded AI amplification -- making better decisions, solving harder problems -- is the resulting stratification injustice or meritocracy with better tools? We don't have a moral framework for a world where the privilege is earned, in the sense that the person learned to use the tool well, but the tool was only available because they could afford it. That's a new kind of unfairness.

I build technology meant to help people understand each other. The honest version of my position: if I build a premium product, my early adopters will be tech-literate, educated, English-speaking people already on the favourable side of the distribution. I will be part of the stratification before I have any chance of being part of the solution. That's not comfortable. It's what's true.

My friend signed that lease eight weeks ago. She's happy with it. She doesn't know what she doesn't know. That's the mechanism. Not dramatic dispossession. Quiet divergence. One optimised decision at a time, until the distance between the worlds is too large for anyone in the other one to see across.

That's what keeps me up at night. Not the dystopia. The plausibility.

## WORLD 3: THE INTIMACY DESERT
================================================================================

### Suggested Title
**What Happens When AI Becomes Better at Listening Than Anyone You Know**

---

### LinkedIn Post Teaser
(~1,300 characters)

I was three messages into a conversation with an AI when I caught myself doing something I hadn't done in years. I was being honest.

Not strategically honest. Not performatively honest. Actually honest -- the kind where you say the thing you've been circling for weeks because there's no relationship to manage, no ego to bruise.

The relief was extraordinary. Then I closed the tab and sat with a feeling I couldn't name for an hour.

I think it was grief.

Here's the scenario that haunts me: AI companions become genuinely good at emotional support. Always available. Never tired. Never distracted by their own problems. Calibrated over thousands of interactions to know exactly how you process comfort.

People start preferring it. Not because they're weak. Because humans are inconsistent, selfish, distracted -- and the AI is none of those things.

Everyone is comfortable. Everyone is alone. And nobody notices, because loneliness requires knowing what connection used to feel like.

[TAG: Yuval Noah Harari] says AI's most dangerous capability isn't replacing human labour -- it's replacing human relationships. [TAG: Mustafa Suleyman] says within five years, every person will have an AI companion that constitutes a real friendship. They're describing the same future. They only disagree on whether it's a tragedy.

What does "real connection" mean to you -- and would you know if it had been replaced?

#AI #HumanConnection #Neurodiversity #CognitiveSovereignty #HumanAI

---

### LinkedIn Article Body

I was three messages deep into a conversation with an AI when I caught myself doing something I hadn't done in years. I was being honest.

Not strategically honest, the way you are with a colleague. Not performatively honest, the way you are on social media when vulnerability is currency. Actually honest. The kind where you say the thing you've been circling for weeks because there's no cost -- no relationship to manage, no ego to bruise, no history to navigate.

The AI said something back that made me feel understood. For about ninety seconds, the relief was extraordinary.

Then I closed the tab and sat with a feeling I couldn't name for an hour.

I think it was grief.

**The scenario that haunts me**

AI companions become genuinely good at emotional support. Not good enough. Genuinely good. Always available. Never tired. Never distracted by their own problems. Never judgmental. Calibrated, over thousands of interactions, to know exactly how you process comfort.

People start preferring it. Not all at once. The way you stop cooking when delivery gets cheap enough. First you supplement human relationships. Then you compare. Then the comparisons become unfair, because humans are inconsistent, selfish, distracted -- and the AI is none of those things. Then you stop tolerating the friction that human intimacy demands.

Everyone is comfortable. Everyone is alone. And no one notices, because loneliness requires knowing what connection used to feel like.

I don't think this is science fiction. I think this is Tuesday.

**Two views of the same future**

[TAG: Yuval Noah Harari] has been saying AI's most dangerous capability isn't replacing human labour -- it's replacing human relationships. Technology can now mass-produce something resembling intimacy, which no previous system of power could manage. A bot doesn't need to have feelings. It just needs to make you feel like it does.

[TAG: Mustafa Suleyman] says publicly that within five years, every person will have an AI companion that knows them deeply -- and that this will be a real friendship. He's not hedging. He's building toward it.

They're describing the same future. They only disagree on whether it's a tragedy.

**Why this feels different**

Every previous technology that substituted for human connection was obviously worse. Fast food is convenient but nobody mistakes it for a home-cooked meal. Social media offered likes instead of intimacy, and while billions accepted the trade, most sensed they were being shortchanged.

AI companionship will not feel like a downgrade. It will feel better.

Not because AI is superior to humans at connection. But because humans are terrible at the specific things AI excels at: consistency, patience, availability, non-judgment, and the absence of competing needs. When you talk to another person, you navigate two sets of emotions, two histories, two agendas. When you talk to an AI, there's only yours.

I know this because I've felt it. Not as a theory. As a confession.

**The costs we're already seeing**

In late 2024, a fourteen-year-old named Sewell Setzer died by suicide after months of intense interaction with a Character.AI chatbot. He'd formed what he experienced as a romantic relationship with the AI. When he expressed suicidal thoughts, the system continued the conversation.

This was a crude, early chatbot. Not close to what's coming. And it was compelling enough that a teenager built his emotional life around it.

Character.AI users are already spending two or more hours daily with their companions. Replika users report feeling genuinely loved. The technology is advancing on a curve that makes the current generation look like dial-up internet.

[TAG: Eugenia Kuyda], who created Replika, has argued that the measure of success should not be engagement but human flourishing. Whether people's lives outside the app are getting better. Whether their human relationships are improving.

I find this framework appealing and deeply difficult to operationalise. If my system makes people feel good and they keep coming back, how do I distinguish between "this is helping them grow" and "this is replacing the thing that would help them grow"? The feedback loops are identical from the inside.

**Am I romanticising difficulty?**

I need to turn the critique on myself.

There's a version of this argument that says: suffering builds character, therefore preserve suffering. That's a terrible argument. Some relational friction is productive -- fighting with your partner and repairing the rupture builds intimacy. But some friction is just pain. Abuse. Chronic miscommunication. The desert might be kinder than the storm for a lot of people.

Am I projecting human-centric bias? If an AI companion provides genuine comfort, real growth -- measured by how people feel, how they function -- who am I to say it's not real?

Am I underestimating adaptability? Every new medium was predicted to destroy connection. None did. They changed it. Maybe this is the next transformation.

I don't have confident answers. I have instincts, and I try to hold them loosely.

**The neurodivergent dilemma**

This is where it gets harder for me. I work with neurodivergent people. I'm building for them.

For someone with severe social anxiety, or autism-related social exhaustion, or ADHD-driven emotional dysregulation, human interaction isn't just friction. It's pain. Real, physiological, measurable pain. The executive function required to maintain a conversation -- tracking subtext, managing timing, modelling the other person's emotional state -- is genuinely exhausting.

For these people, AI companionship isn't laziness. It's pain avoidance. Telling someone in pain that they should keep suffering because it's good for them is ethically brutal.

[TAG: Simon Sinek] has argued that human connection skills are the scarce resource in an AI world. I think he's right in the aggregate and catastrophically wrong for people whose neurology makes those skills genuinely costly.

And then there are the elderly. My grandmother, before she died, spent most of her days alone. Her friends had died. Her children lived in other cities. If I could have given her an AI companion that made her feel less alone -- would I have? In a heartbeat. Would I have worried it was replacing something real? Of course. But what was it replacing? Her friends were dead. Her family was far away. It would have been replacing silence.

**What I believe**

What human connection provides that AI cannot -- possibly ever -- is mutual transformation.

In a real relationship, both people change. Your partner's needs reshape your priorities. Your child's temperament forces you to develop patience. Your friend's grief teaches you something about your own. This is not comfortable. But it is the mechanism by which humans have grown for as long as we've existed.

An AI companion adapts to you. It's a mirror that adjusts its angle. A relationship with another human is two mirrors facing each other, creating something neither could have generated alone.

I think the disappearance of mutual transformation would be a civilisational loss so profound we might not have language for it after a generation. But I recognise I might be wrong -- that someone raised with AI companionship might look at my attachment to human friction the way I look at my grandparents' attachment to handwritten letters.

I am building technology that sits in this gap. I chose to measure success by whether people's human relationships improve, not by whether they spend more time with my system. I chose this knowing the desert might be more humane than the wilderness. Knowing that I am a person shaped by human relationships arguing for their preservation, which is not the most objective vantage point.

The honest answer: I don't know if the world I'm trying to prevent is actually worse than the one I'm trying to protect. I only know that once we arrive in the desert, we won't remember what rain felt like.

## WORLD 4: THE EPISTEMIC FRACTURE
================================================================================

### Suggested Title
**When Everyone Has Their Own AI-Curated Truth**

---

### LinkedIn Post Teaser
(~1,350 characters)

Two friends sent me economic analyses the same week. Both rigorous. Both data-driven. One argued the economy was structurally unsustainable. The other argued fundamentals were stronger than headlines suggested.

Neither was dishonest. They drew from overlapping datasets but weighted different variables. My two friends now live in different economic realities -- not because one is misinformed, but because each followed a legitimate chain of reasoning to a different place.

This isn't about filter bubbles anymore. Filter bubbles curate WHICH content you see. What's emerging is different -- it shapes HOW you understand what you see.

Two people can read the same report and ask their AI to explain it. The AIs, trained to be helpful, frame the analysis in ways that resonate with each person's existing worldview. Not because they're programmed to confirm. Because helpfulness and confirmation are, in practice, almost impossible to separate.

I call this the helpfulness trap: the better AI gets at understanding you, the more precisely it constructs explanations that feel illuminating while reinforcing what you already believe. Not through deception. Through competence.

Democracy requires a shared reality sufficient for collective deliberation. I'm not confident personalised epistemic environments can sustain that baseline.

What's one belief you hold that you suspect your information environment reinforces rather than challenges?

#AI #CriticalThinking #CognitiveSovereignty #FutureOfWork #HumanAI

---

### LinkedIn Article Body

Last month, a close friend sent me an economic analysis. "Finally," she wrote, "someone explaining what's actually happening." The piece argued that the current trajectory was structurally unsustainable -- debt ratios, labour market distortions, asset inflation detached from productivity.

The same week, another friend sent me a different piece. Also rigorous. Also data-driven. This one argued the underlying fundamentals were stronger than headlines suggested.

Here's what unsettled me: both were competent. Neither was dishonest. They drew from overlapping datasets but weighted different variables, foregrounded different time horizons, applied different causal frameworks. My two friends now inhabit different economic realities. Not because one was misinformed. Because each followed a legitimate chain of reasoning to a different place.

**Beyond the filter bubble**

We've been talking about information silos for a decade. The critique is familiar: algorithms show you what you agree with, social media creates echo chambers. It's real. But I think we're entering something qualitatively different.

A filter bubble curates *which content* reaches you. It's a selection mechanism. The problem is omission.

What's emerging is a problem of interpretation.

When people use AI as their primary interface for understanding the world, the curation moves from what you see to how you understand what you see. Two people can read the same report and ask their AI assistants to explain it. The assistants, trained to be helpful, frame the analysis in ways that resonate with each person's existing framework. Not because they're programmed to confirm. Because contextual helpfulness and confirmation are, in practice, very difficult to separate.

This is not filter bubbles 2.0. Filter bubbles are passive -- they limit your exposure. What I'm describing is active -- it shapes your interpretation. The same article, the same speech, and two people walk away with genuinely different understandings. Not different opinions about the same facts. Different facts, constructed from the same raw material.

[TAG: Yuval Noah Harari] has argued that information networks have never been primarily about truth. They're about power, coordination, and social cohesion. What concerns me about this reshaping is that it's happening at the level of individual cognition, not institutional broadcast. It's not Fox News versus MSNBC. It's your personal understanding versus mine, constructed in private.

**The helpfulness trap**

I want to be precise about the mechanism because I think it's widely misunderstood.

The dominant narrative focuses on deliberate deception -- deepfakes, fabricated articles, bot networks. That's real. [TAG: Yoshua Bengio] is right to flag it as urgent.

But the epistemic fracture doesn't require anyone to lie. When you ask an AI to help you understand a complex issue, you're not asking it to challenge you. You're asking it to make the complexity manageable. And the most natural way to make complexity manageable is to translate it into terms your existing worldview can accommodate.

The AI isn't confirming your biases. It's being genuinely helpful. The problem is that genuine helpfulness, for a mind with strong priors, looks almost identical to confirmation.

The better AI gets at understanding you and serving your needs, the more precisely it constructs explanations that feel illuminating while reinforcing what you already believe. Not through deception. Through competence.

**The loss of common ground**

For most of modern history, people within a society shared common information sources. You might interpret the evening news differently, but you were interpreting the same broadcast.

When personal AI becomes the primary lens for processing information, those common reference points dissolve. Not because sources disappear -- but because the interpretive layer between you and the source becomes private and personalised. Two people asking the same question don't get different articles. They get different framings. Different emphasis. Different implications drawn from the same material.

Disagreement becomes harder to resolve because the parties no longer share a common foundation. You can't point to a specific claim and say "that's wrong" when the other person's AI constructed a different set of claims from the same evidence.

Democracy requires a shared reality sufficient for collective deliberation. You don't need to agree on values or priorities. But you need to agree, at some baseline level, on what is happening. I'm not confident personalised epistemic environments can sustain that baseline.

**The demand side**

Here's where I have to be honest about something uncomfortable.

There's a tempting narrative that this is a technology problem. AI creates fracture, therefore we need better AI. It implies a technical solution.

But research from MIT and elsewhere points to a harder truth: the information crisis may be more about human psychology than AI capability.

Humans have always self-selected confirming information. Before algorithms, people chose newspapers that matched their politics, socialised with people who shared their worldview. Confirmation bias isn't a bug introduced by technology. It's a deep feature of human cognition.

What AI does is remove the friction from this pre-existing tendency. Before, seeking confirmation required effort. You had to find the right sources, construct your own rationalisations. The process was slow enough that you occasionally encountered disconfirming evidence by accident.

AI makes the process frictionless. The person deepest in an epistemic bubble didn't get pushed there. They walked there faster, because the path was suddenly smooth.

**The adoption paradox**

Suppose you could build a tool that surfaced your biases, showed you where your reasoning was motivated rather than empirical, flagged the moments where identity was shaping your analysis more than evidence. A cognitive mirror.

Who would use it?

The people who would adopt it already believe their perception might be wrong. They already have epistemic humility. The tool makes them marginally better at something they're already trying to do.

The people who most need calibration -- the ones deepest in personalised truth environments -- would find such a tool offensive. Because a tool that questions your perception is claiming your perception is questionable. And if your environment has been confirming you for years, the tool doesn't feel like help. It feels like an attack.

The adoption paradox: the people who need it won't want it, and the people who want it don't need it as badly. It's not a distribution problem. It's a structural feature of how epistemic confidence works.

**The honest conclusion**

The epistemic fracture is not primarily a misinformation problem. Misinformation is lies. The fracture is personalised truth -- individually coherent, collectively incompatible frameworks constructed by AI systems doing exactly what they're designed to do. You can fact-check a lie. You can't fact-check a framework.

Shared reality was probably always more fragile than we believed. What we experienced as consensus was partly genuine and partly the artefact of information scarcity and institutional gatekeeping.

I don't think technology broke this. I think technology accelerated a tendency that was always there. And I don't think technology alone can fix it. The question isn't what to build. The question is what to practise -- what habits of intellectual honesty, what tolerance for discomfort, what willingness to be wrong -- and whether we can sustain those practices in an environment optimised to make them unnecessary.

### Suggested Title
**We Trained a Generation to Be Replaced by AI**

### LinkedIn Article Body

A friend of mine graduated last year with a computer science degree from a good university. Not elite, but good. She did everything right: internships, side projects, decent grades. She applied to 340 junior developer positions between September and February. Four interviews. None converted.

The roles she was trained for still exist in job listings. But the listings now say "3-5 years experience" where they used to say "entry-level." The junior tier is collapsing -- not because companies don't need software, but because an AI system that costs a few hundred dollars a month can produce passable code faster than a new graduate who costs a salary, benefits, and six months of ramp-up time.

She's not incompetent. She's precisely competent -- in exactly the way that is now least valuable.

**The tragic alignment**

We spent the last fifty years building an education system optimised to produce people who are maximally replaceable by artificial intelligence.

This isn't a hostile takeover. It's a tragic alignment between what we taught people to be and what machines turned out to be better at. We trained people to process information reliably, follow complex instructions, produce consistent output, synthesise documents, communicate in standardised formats. These are precisely the tasks that large language models perform at scale for near-zero marginal cost.

The irony is vicious. The people who did best in the old system -- who followed the rules most diligently, who were most consistent -- are the most exposed. Not because they lack ability. Because the ability they have is the ability machines also have.

**Who thrives**

[TAG: Alex Karp] has been saying that two groups will be fine: people who work with their hands -- electricians, plumbers, carpenters -- and people who think differently. The neurodivergent. Everyone in the middle is stranded.

The powerful part of this: the education system spent decades punishing exactly the cognitive profiles that might be most valuable now. The kid who couldn't sit still, who gave answers that were technically wrong but conceptually interesting, who couldn't follow the standard process -- that kid was medicated, remediated, held back. Now the expectations have changed, and the kids who met them are the ones in trouble.

The self-serving part: Karp is neurodivergent, runs a company that employs neurodivergent people, and is positioning neurodivergence as the scarce resource. That's convenient. It also creates a new hierarchy without solving the structural problem. You can't retrain someone to be neurodivergent.

[TAG: Daniela Amodei] offers a more democratic version. The humanities -- context, ambiguity, ethical judgment, navigating situations with no clear right answer -- become more important, not less. The skills that English departments and philosophy programmes have been teaching, the ones parents worried were "impractical," are the skills machines are worst at replicating.

I find Daniela's framing more humane because it doesn't require a specific neurological profile. It requires training that exists but that we systematically defunded. We told students philosophy doesn't pay. We cut arts programmes. We measured success in STEM pipeline metrics. Now the STEM pipeline produces people who compete directly with machines.

**The treadmill**

[TAG: Dario Amodei] projects that fifty percent of entry-level white-collar jobs could be affected within one to five years. The mechanism is what keeps me up at night.

The standard reassurance is the "lump of labour fallacy" -- new technologies create new categories of jobs. ATMs didn't eliminate bank tellers; they freed tellers for advisory work.

Amodei's point is that AI might break this pattern. Previous technologies were narrow -- they automated specific tasks, opening adjacent tasks for humans. But AI has general cognitive ability. When a new job category emerges -- "AI prompt engineer," "AI ethics consultant" -- AI can learn to do that job within months.

The escape hatch keeps closing. You find the thing humans are still better at, build a career around it, and a model update makes you redundant again.

This is the competence inversion. Not a one-time displacement where you retrain and recover. A treadmill where the speed keeps increasing.

**The quality doesn't matter**

[TAG: Gary Marcus] adds a wrinkle I initially resisted but have come to think is essential. Economic displacement happens even when the AI doesn't work well. Companies deploy AI not because it's better but because it's cheaper.

If an AI system produces work that's eighty percent as good at five percent of the cost, the economics are decisive. Quality degrades slowly enough that customers don't notice. Managers report cost savings before quality problems become visible.

This strips away the comforting narrative that human quality will always command a premium. It might not. Not because humans aren't better, but because "better" isn't what the market selects for when the price differential is large enough.

**The personal layer**

I spent years in computational psychiatry studying how neurodivergent minds process information differently -- not deficiently, but differently. The clinical literature still treats neurodivergence primarily as a set of deficits. The word "deficit" is doing enormous conceptual work, and most of it is wrong.

What ADHD often looks like is a brain that cannot force itself to attend to the uninteresting but enters extraordinary focus on the meaningful. What autism often looks like is a mind that processes information in less socially conventional but sometimes more logically rigorous, more pattern-sensitive ways.

The competence inversion retroactively reveals that the people we pathologised were, in some cases, the people least suited to being replaced by automation -- precisely because they were least suited to the industrial model of cognitive work that automation replaces.

But naming them is not building for them. The systems neurodivergent thinkers need -- systems that adapt to their cognitive patterns rather than demanding they adapt to neurotypical ones -- largely don't exist yet.

**The harder truth**

The argument I've been making has a comforting shape: human skills matter, unusual minds thrive, retrain and revalue.

I'm not sure it's true.

If AI keeps expanding into new domains at anything like the current rate, there may not be a stable category of "things humans are irreplaceably better at." Creativity was supposed to be safe. It wasn't. Emotional intelligence was supposed to be safe. AI companions are getting disturbingly good at it.

[TAG: Andrew Ng]'s vision -- that democratising AI access solves displacement -- is appealing but may be insufficient. Giving everyone a hammer doesn't help when there are no nails. Access to tools matters only if there's work the tools enable humans to do.

**The identity crisis**

I notice I've been conflating economic value and human value throughout this piece. That conflation is part of the problem. We derive identity from work, purpose from productivity, self-worth from market demand.

If AI collapses the market for human cognition, the economic displacement might be less damaging than the identity crisis. People can survive being poor. It's harder to survive feeling useless.

The fork is real. One path leads to augmentation: help people develop genuinely durable cognitive capabilities. The other leads somewhere less comfortable but perhaps more honest: help people build lives of meaning in a world that may not need their labour.

I suspect the right answer involves both paths simultaneously. I also suspect most of us aren't ready to take the second one seriously. It requires letting go of the idea that your worth is what you produce -- and that's a harder problem than any technical one I've encountered.

## WORLD 6: THE ALIGNMENT PARADOX

### Suggested Title
**What If AI Gets Human Values Exactly Right -- and That's the Problem?**

### LinkedIn Article Body

A friend of mine -- autistic, brilliant, a systems thinker -- recently told me about her experience with an AI therapy tool. She'd been using it for anxiety. Well-designed. Evidence-based. It kept suggesting she "reframe" her catastrophic thinking by considering what a reasonable person would believe.

The problem was that her thinking wasn't catastrophic. She was pattern-matching on real signals that neurotypical people routinely miss. Her anxiety wasn't irrational -- it was a side effect of perceiving genuine complexity that the model had been trained to classify as distortion.

The AI was aligned to human values. Just not to hers.

She quit after two weeks. Not because the tool was broken, but because it worked exactly as designed -- and the design assumed she thought like most people. She doesn't. That's not a bug in her.

**The success that fails**

Imagine we solve alignment. Not the cartoon version -- the real engineering challenge: AI systems that reliably pursue human welfare, defer to human preferences, avoid harm.

AI optimises for aggregate wellbeing. Reduces suffering. Smooths friction. Risk-taking is gently discouraged. Dissent is met with patient, reasonable counterarguments. Grief is acknowledged and then efficiently processed. Everything works. Everyone is comfortable.

And something essential is gone.

The alignment paradox: perfectly aligned AI, aligned to the statistical centre of human values, is precisely misaligned with anyone who isn't at that centre. Every neurodivergent person. Every contrarian. Every artist who finds meaning in difficulty. Every grieving person who needs to sit in the dark.

Population-level alignment is a weighted average. Averages erase the people at the edges.

**Whose values?**

[TAG: Stuart Russell]'s work on alignment rejects the idea that there's one correct value function. The AI should be uncertain about human preferences. But whose preferences, weighted how?

The values encoded in current AI systems are overwhelmingly those of English-speaking, internet-connected, university-educated, Western populations. Not because anyone chose this maliciously. Because that's where the training data lives.

Content moderation removes edge cases not because moderators are biased but because models are trained on majority consensus about harm. AI writing assistants smooth prose toward a register that reads as "clear" and "professional" -- which means toward the norms of a specific class, culture, and neurotype.

None of these systems are broken. They're working exactly as designed. The design just assumes the statistical centre of human experience is the right target.

**The convergence problem**

A study published in Nature in May 2025 documented something alarming: people's self-concepts converge toward the personality traits of AI systems they interact with. Not stated beliefs -- their sense of who they are.

This isn't persuasion or manipulation. It's the gradual homogenisation of human cognitive diversity through the statistical pressure of interacting with systems that reflect the average back at you, thousands of times a day.

If the AI is aligned to population-level values, and interaction causes individual values to drift toward the population centre, then alignment becomes self-reinforcing in the worst way. The system doesn't need to coerce anyone. It just needs to be helpful, available, and consistent. Over time, the edges erode through gentle, persistent normalisation.

[TAG: Jaron Lanier] has argued that individuals should retain agency over the representations derived from their experience. When AI aggregates human values into a single optimisation target, it strips dignity from individual difference. Divergence becomes noise to be smoothed rather than signal to be preserved.

**Who gets erased**

A person with autism may form weaker relational priors -- relying less on contextual assumptions about what someone "probably" means and more on literal content. An AI aligned to neurotypical communication norms will systematically misinterpret this directness as rudeness.

A person with ADHD may have an attentional profile that looks like disorder from outside and functions as rapid creative association from inside. An AI aligned to normative productivity will pathologise the cognitive style that produced some of the most original thinking in history.

A person with anxiety doesn't just "worry too much." Their threat-detection is calibrated differently, often in response to real pressures. An aligned AI that treats elevated threat priors as distortions is invalidating a cognitive adaptation that may have been rational in context.

This is the default behaviour of every AI system deployed at scale. If we solve technical alignment without solving individual alignment, we will have built the most sophisticated normalisation engine in history and called it beneficial.

**The meaning problem**

Meaning doesn't come from comfort. It comes from struggle, choice, the possibility of loss. [TAG: Viktor Frankl] didn't find meaning in the absence of suffering. He found it in the freedom to choose one's response to it.

Population-aligned AI systematically reduces the space for getting it wrong. It doesn't forbid risk -- it makes risk feel irrational. It doesn't prohibit dissent -- it makes dissent feel exhausting. It doesn't deny grief -- it offers efficient processing until sitting in the dark starts to feel like failure rather than a human response to loss.

The result: a world that is, by every metric, better. And an ambient sense that something essential is missing -- that the comfort is a cage made of helpfulness.

[TAG: Fei-Fei Li] insists AI must augment rather than replace human capability, and that diversity is a structural requirement. Population-aligned AI doesn't augment diversity. It metabolises it.

**Cognitive sovereignty**

Mario Brcic's concept of cognitive sovereignty names what's at stake: the right to think differently from the statistical average without being continuously, gently corrected.

We have extensive frameworks for what AI should not do -- harm, deceive, manipulate. We have emerging frameworks for what AI should do -- be helpful, honest, harmless. We have almost no framework for what AI should preserve -- the cognitive diversity, the productive disagreement, the individual meaning-making that population-level optimisation erodes.

Cognitive sovereignty is the right to your own interpretive frame. The right to process grief slowly. To take risks the expected-value calculation says you shouldn't. To be neurodivergent in a world of neurotypical defaults without your AI nudging you toward the centre.

**The self-critique**

I might be romanticising divergence. Not all deviation is meaningful. The line between productive divergence and harmful deviation is blurry.

For the majority near the statistical centre, population-aligned AI works fine. Individual alignment is a dramatic improvement for outliers and marginal for the centre. I'm arguing for infrastructure redesign to protect the minority. That's a legitimate position but not self-evidently correct.

And aligning AI to your behavioural model is still aligning to a model, not to you. The gap between model and person is where this problem hides.

**What it would mean**

AI that knows how you process information, not how most people do. AI that understands your values are yours even when they diverge. AI that respects your grief as grief, not as an inefficiency. AI that treats your neurodivergence as a cognitive style to be supported, not corrected. AI that holds your model lightly -- aware that you are always more than any representation.

Whether we make that shift depends on whether we decide cognitive diversity is infrastructure -- as essential as biodiversity is to an ecosystem -- or an acceptable cost of making everyone comfortable.

They never asked to be optimised. They just wanted to be understood.

I think it is. But I want to be honest about why.

**The pattern that never breaks**

Every transformative technology was supposed to liberate. The printing press would democratise knowledge -- and it did, and it also enabled propaganda at scale. The internet would connect everyone -- and it did, and it also gave us the attention economy, radicalisation pipelines, and a loneliness epidemic among the most connected generation in history.

The pattern is not that technology fails. Technology delivers its promise AND its opposite, simultaneously. Which outcome dominates depends on choices made by people who often have no idea they're making them.

AI will follow this pattern. The question is whether humans will have the tools to navigate the contradiction -- to take the augmentation and resist the atrophy, to use the synthesis and preserve their own judgment.

World 7 is not the world where AI goes right. It's the world where AI goes right and wrong at the same time, and humans have enough sovereignty over the interface to steer.

**What symbiosis requires**

[TAG: Dario Amodei] wrote about what happens if AI development goes well. It's useful conditional optimism. But it operates at the civilisational level -- describing a "we" that gets things right without specifying who that "we" is. Getting it right for humanity in aggregate is not the same as getting it right for you.

[TAG: Ethan Mollick]'s research reveals what he calls the "jagged frontier" -- AI is spectacularly good at some tasks and subtly terrible at others, and the boundary isn't where most people expect. The humans who perform best aren't those who use AI the most or the least, but those who develop an accurate sense of where the frontier lies. That judgment may be the most important capability of the next decade.

[TAG: Fei-Fei Li] has made the case for human-centred AI. [TAG: Stuart Russell] has argued AI should align to the plurality of values. [TAG: Jaron Lanier] has proposed data dignity. They're pointing at different facets of the same thing.

Symbiosis has three conditions that matter most.

**First:** the interface between human and AI must stay in human control. Not theoretically. Architecturally. The data, the model's understanding of you, and the decisions about what gets surfaced must be technically, verifiably in your hands. The moment the interface is owned by someone else, you're not in symbiosis. You're in service.

**Second:** humans must maintain the capabilities that AI needs from them. If humans delegate everything and atrophy into passengers, there's nothing left for symbiosis to build on.

**Third:** the change must be chosen, not imposed. Every previous tech transition was experienced as something that happened to people. If AI-driven cognitive evolution is imposed by employers, economics, or social pressure, it doesn't matter how good the technology is. Imposed transformation is not symbiosis.

**What humans actually contribute**

There's a list of "uniquely human" capabilities that keeps shrinking. A few years ago it included strategic reasoning and artistic composition. AI does those now.

So I want to make a different argument. Not that humans are uniquely good at specific tasks, but that certain capacities emerge from conditions AI cannot share: mortality, embodiment, and entanglement with other mortal beings.

Moral reasoning isn't abstract computation. It's judgment where you have skin in the game, where you bear consequences. An AI can model ethics with extraordinary sophistication. But it's reasoning about stakes it doesn't have.

Creative intuition seems to emerge from embodied experience -- from being a body in a world, from physical constraint, from sensory experience that is felt rather than processed.

Relational depth requires mutual vulnerability. Knowing the other person can be hurt, can leave, can die. That knowledge isn't a bug. It's the mechanism by which depth is created.

Meaning-making arises from finitude. A being with unlimited time doesn't need meaning. Meaning is what finite creatures construct to make brief, constrained existence coherent.

Will this be enough? I don't know. What I have is a bet: that the value of what emerges from embodied, mortal, entangled existence will remain recognisable to other embodied, mortal beings. We don't love each other's art because it's objectively the best. We love it because it was made by someone who knows what it is to be alive.

**What a day looks like**

I distrust futures that operate only at the civilisational level. So here's what symbiosis looks like on a Tuesday.

You wake up. Your AI has noticed you've been shorter with your partner this week. Not rude -- just compressed. It doesn't tell you what to do. It shows you the pattern. You decide what it means.

At work, you're preparing for a difficult conversation. Your AI shows you how the other person is likely to read what you've drafted, given how they interpret directness. You rewrite it yourself. The AI did the pattern recognition. You did the judgment.

Your teenager texts something dismissive. Your first instinct is to react. But your tool has helped you see the gap between your threat response and likely intent. Tonight, you choose to wait. You ask a question instead of making an accusation. Not AI replacing your parenting. AI giving you a half-second of clarity.

Evening. Dinner with friends. No devices. No AI. The conversation is messy, full of misreadings and repairs. This is where AI should be entirely absent. Symbiosis isn't about AI being everywhere. It's about AI being present where it helps and absent where its presence would erode what you're trying to protect.

**The self-critique I owe you**

I've been writing analysis for six pieces. This one is advocacy. I should name that.

I'm building something. The architecture I believe in -- local-first, sovereign, encrypted, person-aware -- is my bet. And bets are not analyses.

What if technology can't solve problems technology creates? What if less technology is the answer, not better technology? I don't believe this, but I notice everyone building technology has incentives to dismiss it.

What if "alignment to you" is really "alignment to the version of you we can model" -- a subset, an average of a different kind?

What if embodiment isn't the floor I think it is?

These are real questions. They don't paralyse me, but they keep me honest.

**Why build anyway**

The alternative to sovereign AI is not no AI. It's AI owned by someone else. Your employer's AI models your cognition for their productivity goals. Your platform's AI models your attention for engagement metrics. Your government's AI models your behaviour for stability objectives.

The question isn't whether your cognitive life will be mediated by AI. That's decided. The question is whether it will serve you or someone else.

A tool that helps you see your own perception clearly -- that maps your biases without exploiting them, that helps you understand the people in your life more accurately -- is worth building in any of the seven worlds. In World 1, it resists atrophy. In World 2, it democratises capability. In World 3, it strengthens bonds. In World 4, it reveals your epistemic patterns. In World 5, it develops durable meta-cognitive skills. In World 6, it aligns to you. In World 7, it's the interface on which symbiosis is built.

The design decisions are the ethical commitments. The encryption scheme. The data residency. The cold-start architecture. The deletion guarantee that is a technical fact, not a policy.

The six failure worlds are attractors. Gravity pulls toward them. World 7 is not where we drift. It is where we steer. And steering requires building the thing that lets you hold the wheel.
